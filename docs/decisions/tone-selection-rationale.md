# Tone Selection Rationale

**Document Type:** Decision Record
**Decision Date:** January 2026
**Status:** Final

---

## Executive Summary

This document explains the reasoning behind our tone selections for the OSHA safety content targeting volunteer construction workers. The selections are:

| Role | Tone | Rationale Summary |
|------|------|-------------------|
| **Primary** | Tone 2: Been There | Trust-building, memorable stories, validates learning |
| **Runner-up** | Tone 1: Straight Talk | Efficiency, clarity, returning volunteers |
| **Infographics** | Tone 5: Quick Hits | Visual format compatibility, brevity |
| **Reserved** | Tones 3 & 4 | Situational use only |

---

## Why Tone 2 "Been There" is Primary

### The Core Challenge We're Solving

Our audience—volunteers "off the street" with varied educational backgrounds and minimal construction experience—faces a fundamental barrier: **trust**. Before they'll internalize safety information, they need to believe:

1. The person sharing knows what they're talking about
2. That person understands their situation
3. The information is genuinely for their benefit, not just liability protection

### How "Been There" Addresses the Trust Gap

**1. Connection Through Shared Experience**

"Been There" uses first-person narrative ("I learned this the hard way...") that positions the communicator as a peer who has walked the same path. This is fundamentally different from authority-based messaging that creates an "us vs. them" dynamic.

Research on adult learning consistently shows that learners retain information better when it comes from someone they perceive as similar to themselves, not from an authority figure.

**2. Memorable Story Format**

Stories are the oldest and most effective human learning technology. When someone says "The reason they say '3-point contact' is because that's your stability," they're not reciting a rule—they're sharing earned wisdom.

Key retention advantages:
- Narrative structure provides mental "hooks" for recall
- Emotional engagement (even mild) strengthens memory encoding
- Stories naturally answer "why" without lecturing
- Easier to retell to others (peer-to-peer multiplication)

**3. Validates the Learning Curve**

Many volunteers arrive nervous about their inexperience. "Been There" explicitly acknowledges that everyone starts somewhere:

> "When I first started, I thought hard hats were overkill. Then..."

This validation reduces anxiety and resistance. A volunteer who feels embarrassed about not knowing something is less likely to ask questions or admit confusion—both dangerous outcomes on a construction site.

**4. Reduces Resistance to Direction**

Our audience analysis identified that many volunteers "don't like being told what to do." This is a psychological reality, not a flaw. "Been There" routes around this resistance by framing guidance as experience-sharing rather than instruction:

- **Instruction:** "Always maintain 3-point contact on the ladder."
- **Been There:** "The reason they say '3-point contact' is because that's your stability."

Same information, fundamentally different reception.

### What "Been There" Looks Like in Practice

> "First time I picked up a circular saw, nobody told me about kickback. Nearly took out my knee. Here's the thing—the saw wants to jump if it binds. Keep your body to the side, not behind it. And if it starts binding? Let go of the trigger. Don't force it."

This passage:
- Establishes credibility through admitted experience
- Creates urgency through near-miss narrative
- Explains the "why" (kickback physics)
- Provides clear action steps
- Stays conversational and peer-level

## Why Tone 1 "Straight Talk" is Runner-up

### When Efficiency Beats Narrative

"Straight Talk" is the right choice when:

**1. The audience already trusts the source**
- Returning volunteers who've heard the full explanations
- Groups that have worked together before
- Post-rapport situations

**2. Time is genuinely constrained**
- Quick morning briefings
- Tool checkout moments
- Last-minute reminders

**3. The listener prefers directness**
- Rule-followers who want clarity, not stories
- Analytical thinkers who find narrative inefficient
- Experienced tradespeople volunteering their time

### Why It's Not Primary

"Straight Talk" is efficient but not complete. It excels at *what* but struggles with *why*:

| Aspect | Straight Talk | Been There |
|--------|---------------|------------|
| Speed | Faster | Moderate |
| Trust-building | Limited | Strong |
| "Why" explanation | Minimal | Embedded |
| Memory retention | Medium | High |
| First-time volunteers | Adequate | Excellent |
| Returning volunteers | Excellent | Adequate |

For first-time volunteers—our primary audience—"Straight Talk" alone may feel cold and compliance-focused. It works best as a *complement* to "Been There," not a replacement.

### The "Been There → Straight Talk" Sequence

The ideal pattern:
1. First exposure: "Been There" (build trust, explain why)
2. Reinforcement: "Straight Talk" (efficient reminders)

Example sequence:

**Day 1 (Been There):**
> "The reason they say '3-point contact' is because that's your stability. Two hands and a foot, or two feet and a hand—always. The moment you've only got two points, you're one shift away from trouble."

**Day 2 reminder (Straight Talk):**
> "Ladders: three points of contact, always. Belt buckle between the rails. Let's go."

## Why Tone 5 "Quick Hits" is Reserved for Infographics

### The Visual Format Constraint

Infographics, signage, and pocket cards have physical and cognitive constraints that demand extreme brevity:

- **Space limitations:** 25-50 words maximum
- **Glanceability:** Must be understood in seconds
- **Point-of-use context:** Reader is about to (or actively) do the task
- **No opportunity for narrative:** Visual format doesn't support story structure

### What Makes "Quick Hits" Work for Visual Formats

**1. Zero Preamble**
"Quick Hits" assumes prior understanding. It reinforces rather than teaches. This is appropriate for materials used *after* orientation.

**2. Scannable Structure**
Bullets, numbers, and short phrases are optimized for visual scanning:

> 3 points on the ladder.
> Two hands + foot, or two feet + hand.
> Belt buckle between the rails.
> Past the rails = overreaching = move the ladder.

**3. Action-Dense**
Every word earns its place. No context-setting, no explanation, no motivation—just the critical action items.

### Why "Quick Hits" is Not Primary

As a standalone training approach, "Quick Hits" fails our audience:

- **No trust-building:** Feels impersonal
- **No "why":** Rules without reasons trigger resistance
- **Assumes prior knowledge:** Our volunteers have none
- **Not memorable for first exposure:** Too sparse to stick

"Quick Hits" is the *result* of learning, not the *vehicle* for it. A volunteer who has internalized ladder safety through "Been There" messaging can use a "Quick Hits" card as a reminder. A volunteer encountering safety information for the first time through "Quick Hits" alone will retain little.

## Why Tones 3 and 4 Are Not Selected for This Phase

### Tone 3: "Let's Figure This Out"

**What it does well:**
- Engages analytical thinkers
- Builds deep understanding through collaborative exploration
- Effective for resistant groups who dislike being "taught"

**Why it's reserved:**
- **Time-intensive:** Takes longer to deliver than "Been There"
- **Audience mismatch:** Works best with small groups and discussion; our materials are primarily documents and presentations
- **Cognitive load:** Requires more active engagement than some volunteers can provide after travel/early morning

**When to use it anyway:**
- Complex topics that benefit from exploration (e.g., Job Hazard Analysis process)
- Small group discussions with engaged participants
- Post-incident debriefs where understanding matters more than efficiency

### Tone 4: "Real Consequences"

**What it does well:**
- Creates lasting impressions
- Effective for overconfident volunteers
- Appropriate gravitas for genuinely high-stakes topics

**Why it's reserved:**
- **Overuse risk:** Frequent consequence messaging becomes fear-mongering
- **Anxiety amplification:** Nervous volunteers become more nervous, not more careful
- **Calibration problem:** Hard to use "Real Consequences" for minor hazards without losing credibility for major ones

**When to use it anyway:**
- High-risk tasks (falls from height, electrical work, trench entry)
- When complacency is observed
- Post-incident discussions
- Supervisor talking points for serious corrections

### The Situational Matrix

| Tone | Default Use | Reserved For |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| Been There | All standard content | - |
| Straight Talk | Returning volunteers, time-constrained situations | - |
| Quick Hits | All visual/infographic materials | - |
| Let's Figure This Out | - | Complex topics, discussions, resistant groups |
| Real Consequences | - | High-risk tasks, complacency, post-incident |

## Supporting Evidence

### From Audience Analysis

Our audience research identified these key factors that drove tone selection:

| Factor | Implication | Supporting Tone |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| Varied educational backgrounds | Cannot assume reading level or learning style | Been There (accessible narrative) |
| Little construction experience | Need foundational "why" not just rules | Been There (explains reasoning) |
| May be nervous | Need reassurance, not intimidation | Been There (validates learning) |
| "Don't like being told what to do" | Authority-based messaging creates resistance | Been There (peer positioning) |
| Diverse learning styles | Need multiple modalities | Been There + Quick Hits visual |

### From Learning Science

| Principle | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| Stories improve retention | Been There uses narrative structure |
| Peer learning is effective | All tones avoid authority positioning |
| Chunking aids memory | Quick Hits for point-of-use reinforcement |
| "Why" increases compliance | Been There embeds rationale |
| Fear has diminishing returns | Real Consequences reserved for genuine high-stakes |

## Implementation Implications

### Content Production Priority

1. **All written content:** Draft in "Been There" voice first
2. **Infographics/signage:** Use "Quick Hits" format
3. **Returning volunteer materials:** Offer "Straight Talk" alternatives
4. **Supplement library:** Develop "Let's Figure This Out" discussion guides and "Real Consequences" high-risk modules as secondary resources

### Quality Assurance

Every piece of content should be validated against:

- [ ] Would a real volunteer actually say this?
- [ ] Is the action clear?
- [ ] Does it respect the listener's intelligence?
- [ ] Is it free of jargon (or explains jargon immediately)?
- [ ] Could someone remember this on the jobsite?
- [ ] Does it pass the "not a lecture" test?
